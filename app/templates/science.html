About SimLE Science

SimLE Science is an interdisciplinary initiative focused on bridging the gap between science and engineering. Our projects emphasize collaboration among various specialists to achieve innovative scientific goals.

One of the projects realized under SimLE Science is AMBER - Autonomous Modular Biotechnological Experiment on a Rocket, which is carried out in collaboration with NASA Ames Research Center. The aim of the AMBER project is to investigate the impact of suborbital rocket flight on biosynthesis processes, enzyme reaction efficiency, biofilm properties, and mutagenic potential, utilizing a 96 well-plate payload system for testing.

The project also includes experiments with biofilm, which plays a significant role in understanding bacterial behavior under space conditions, potentially contributing to advancements in space medicine and the creation of a microbiome on Mars.

Another key project is the Stardust Microbiology Project, a collaboration with the European, Swedish, and German Space Agencies under the REXUS/BEXUS program. This project focuses on stratospheric balloon experiments to study the microbial flora of the stratosphere and its resistance to cosmic radiation. Stardust's successful missions have already led to groundbreaking findings, including the discovery of previously unknown bacteria from the stratosphere.

Our Journey: The Evolution of SimLE Science

SimLE Science began as an ambitious student initiative driven by a passion for space exploration and scientific research. The projects, such as AMBER and Stardust, have steadily grown from early conceptual stages to highly successful experiments in suborbital flight, microbiology, and radiation studies.

SimLE Science Development Timeline

2015

SimLE Science project established. Early missions focused on reaching the stratosphere and testing experimental systems.

2017

Collaboration with NASA Ames Research Center on AMBER project to study suborbital rocket flight effects on biosynthesis and biofilm properties.

2019

Successful participation in REXUS/BEXUS program with Stardust Microbiology Project, launching microbiological experiments from Esrange Space Center, Sweden.

2020

Launch of microbiological and radiation experiments on stratospheric balloon mission, advancing research into cosmic radiation effects on microbes.

2023

Development of biofilm research continues, alongside the creation of a vacuum chamber prototype for simulating stratospheric conditions.
